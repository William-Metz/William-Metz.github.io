Project information

  • Category: Academic Research
  • Program: The Center for Advanced Research & Studies at Thousand Oaks High School
  • Project date: August 2018 - January 2020

During my final two years of high school, I engaged in an intensive research project at the Center for Advanced Research and Studies. This research sought to test the efficacy of the Neuroevolution of Augmenting Topologies (NEAT) algorithm in optimizing the airfoil lift-drag coefficient, comparing it to both the industry-standard Multidisciplinary Design Analysis and Optimization (MDAO) algorithm and a Genetic Algorithm (GA).

Our experiment used varying degrees of control over the airfoil design—one, six, and 211—and repeated each test 30 times to ensure a statistically significant sample size. Statistical analysis methods, such as ANOVA and Kruskal-Wallis tests, were employed to identify significant differences in the results.

The findings indicated that the NEAT algorithm, when operating with six control variables, outperformed the other methods by orders of magnitude. Despite NEAT's increased runtime and inconsistent results, it demonstrated a superior level of optimization compared to both the MDAO and GA with the same degree of control.

Interestingly, the performance gap between GA and NEAT suggested that the neural network component of NEAT might be more significant than its genetic aspect, providing new insights into the workings of the NEAT algorithm.

These two years of research offered invaluable experience in the use of sophisticated algorithms, statistical analysis, and problem-solving in the field of aerodynamics. It also deepened my understanding of the potential of AI and machine learning in design optimization, highlighting the transformative potential of these technologies in engineering.
